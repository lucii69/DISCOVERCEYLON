Located in Keerimalai at the northernmost tip of the Jaffna Peninsula, the Naguleswaram Temple is one of Sri Lanka’s five ancient Pancha Ishwarams dedicated to Lord Shiva. Historically known as Thirutambaleswaram, it is a site of immense antiquity, with roots reaching back to the classical era. The temple is famous for its proximity to the Keerimalai Springs, a natural mineral-rich pool separated from the sea by a single stone wall, where the fresh water is believed to possess miraculous healing properties.

The temple's name is derived from the legend of the sage Nagula Muni, who was said to have a face resembling a mongoose (Nagula in Sanskrit, Keeri in Tamil) due to an ancient curse. After bathing in the sacred springs, his human features were restored, and he built a shrine in gratitude. Throughout history, the temple has been a victim of conflict, including destruction by the Portuguese in 1621 and damage during the Sri Lankan Civil War, but it has been resiliently restored and expanded, most recently in 2012.

Architecturally, the modern complex features vibrant Dravidian styles, including a towering gopuram and a sanctum housing a rare stone Shiva Lingam. It remains a major pilgrimage site, particularly during the Aadi Amavasai festival, when thousands of devotees gather to perform ancestral rituals and bathe in the springs. The site offers a unique spiritual atmosphere where the sounds of the Indian Ocean blend with the chants of ancient Shaivite traditions.
