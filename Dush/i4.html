Masjid-ul-Abrar (Old Mosque) in Beruwala, Sri Lanka, is one of the most historically significant Islamic sites in the country, often regarded as the oldest mosque on the island. Believed to have been established around 920 AD by Arab spice traders who settled along Sri Lanka’s southwestern coast, the mosque marks the early arrival of Islam to Sri Lanka and stands as a lasting symbol of the island’s rich multicultural heritage.

The mosque’s layout includes a prominent large pond at the front, traditionally used for ritual ablutions before prayer, reflecting the practical and spiritual needs of early Muslim communities. Although the original structure has undergone multiple renovations and expansions over the centuries—with significant refurbishments in the late 20th century to increase capacity—the site continues to serve as an active place of worship and community gathering. Today, it can accommodate thousands of devotees and remains central to religious life in Beruwala

Architecturally, Masjid-ul-Abrar combines Islamic design with local Sri Lankan influences, featuring elegant white walls and traditional decorative patterns that harmonize with its coastal setting. Beyond its architectural appeal, the mosque holds deep cultural and historical importance, symbolizing the long-standing presence of Islam in Sri Lanka and representing centuries of faith, community, and continuity for Muslims across the region.
