Located in the historic town of Nallur, the Nallur Kandaswamy Kovil is the spiritual heart of the Jaffna Peninsula and one of Sri Lanka’s most significant Hindu landmarks. Originally founded in 948 AD, the temple served as the religious center of the Jaffna Kingdom. Although the original complex was destroyed by Portuguese forces in the 17th century, the current structure was rebuilt in 1734 during the Dutch era and has since been expanded into a sprawling, majestic citadel.

The temple is a masterpiece of Dravidian architecture, instantly recognizable by its vibrant golden-ochre walls and soaring gopurams (entrance towers). Its interior is characterized by grand pillared halls, intricate wood carvings, and a serene holy pool. Dedicated to Lord Murugan, the temple enshrines the sacred Vel (divine spear) in its sanctum. A unique tradition of the Kovil is its strict code of conduct; male devotees must enter the inner temple bare-chested as a sign of humility and respect.

As a vibrant cultural hub, Nallur is world-famous for its annual 25-day festival in August, which draws hundreds of thousands of pilgrims. The festival features spectacular processions of the deity on silver vahanams (vehicles) and massive chariots, accompanied by traditional music and acts of deep devotion. Known for its impeccable punctuality and spiritual discipline, the temple remains a timeless symbol of Tamil heritage and religious resilience in northern Sri Lanka.
