The Jami Ul-Alfar Mosque, commonly known as the Red Mosque, is one of Colombo, Sri Lanka’s most distinctive and historic Islamic landmarks. Located in the bustling Pettah district, the mosque was commissioned by the local Indian Muslim community and constructed between 1908 and 1909 to serve as a central place of worship for daily prayers and the Friday Jummah gathering. Its vibrant red-and-white candy-striped exterior makes it instantly recognizable and a popular attraction for both worshippers and visitors exploring the city.

Architecturally, the Red Mosque is a remarkable example of Indo-Saracenic design, blending elements of Indo-Islamic, Gothic revival, and Neo-Classical styles. The structure’s ornate patterns, striking facades, and towering minarets—with domes shaped evocatively like pomegranates—create a visually stunning effect against Colombo’s urban backdrop. This unique style has led some to compare it to the Jamek Mosque in Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia

Beyond its architectural appeal, Jami Ul-Alfar Mosque remains a vibrant center of spiritual life for Sri Lankan Muslims. Over the years, it has been expanded to accommodate thousands of worshippers, reflecting the growth of the community it serves. The mosque also holds symbolic importance as part of Colombo’s multicultural heritage and was once used by sailors as a navigational landmark due to its visibility from the harbor.
